The 2007-2008 financial crisis, or Global Financial Crisis (GFC), was a severe worldwide economic crisis that occurred in the early 21st century. It was the most serious financial crisis since the Great Depression of the 1930s. The crisis resulted in a severe global economic downturn, the Great Recession, and led to the failure or near-failure of several major financial institutions.

The primary catalyst for the crisis was the bursting of the United States housing bubble, which peaked in 2006. For years leading up to the crisis, banks and financial institutions engaged in predatory lending practices, offering subprime mortgages to borrowers with poor credit histories. These high-risk loans were then bundled together and sold to investors worldwide as mortgage-backed securities (MBS) and collateralized debt obligations (CDO). Rating agencies often gave these complex financial instruments unwarranted high ratings, making them appear as safe investments.

When housing prices began to decline and interest rates rose, many homeowners defaulted on their mortgages. The value of MBS and CDOs plummeted, causing massive losses for financial institutions globally. The inter-connected nature of the global financial system meant that the crisis quickly spread from the US housing market to banks in Europe and Asia.

In September 2008, the crisis reached a climax with the collapse of Lehman Brothers, a major global financial services firm. Lehman's bankruptcy, the largest in US history, caused panic in the financial markets and led to a severe credit freeze. Banks stopped lending to each other, fearing counterparty risk. Other major institutions, such as Bear Stearns, Merrill Lynch, and AIG, also faced imminent collapse and either merged with other banks or received massive government bailouts.

In response to the crisis, the US government implemented several emergency measures. The Troubled Asset Relief Program (TARP), signed into law in October 2008, authorized the Treasury to purchase up to $700 billion of toxic assets from financial institutions to stabilize the banking system. The Federal Reserve also took unprecedented actions, lowering interest rates to near-zero and implementing quantitative easing (QE) to inject liquidity into the economy.

The crisis had profound economic consequences worldwide. Global stock markets crashed, and many countries experienced severe recessions. Unemployment rates soared, and millions of people lost their homes to foreclosure. The crisis also led to a sovereign debt crisis in several European countries, most notably Greece, Ireland, Portugal, and Spain.

In the aftermath of the crisis, significant regulatory reforms were introduced to prevent a similar event from occurring in the future. In the US, the Dodd-Frank Wall Street Reform and Consumer Protection Act was passed in 2010. The act aimed to increase transparency and reduce risks in the financial system. It established new regulatory bodies, such as the Financial Stability Oversight Council and the Consumer Financial Protection Bureau. Additionally, the Volcker Rule restricted banks from making certain speculative investments with their own funds. Internationally, the Basel III framework was introduced, requiring banks to maintain higher capital reserves and improve risk management practices.
